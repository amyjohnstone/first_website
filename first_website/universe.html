The Universe

The most widely accepted theory for the origin of the Universe is the Big Bang Theory - no, not the TV show. In the first few moments of its existence highly energetic particles collided, merged, decoupled, you name it they probably did it. Over the next few million years stars and galaxies started to form.


Fast-forward 14 billion years and here we are. The Universe is still expanding, and the expansion is getting faster and faster. By expansion we mean the space between stuff not the stuff itself so don’t worry you’re not going to get fat.


That being said, there is still possibilities for collisions between galaxies. For example, our home galaxy the Milky Way is predicted to collide and merge with our neighbour Andromeda in a few billion years’ time.


What about the fate of the Universe?

Ultimately, the fate of the Universe depends on its density. One popular theory is the Big Crunch where expansion eventually stops and the Universe retreats back on itself. Another theory is the Big Rip where expansion continues indefinitely until the space between atoms becomes so large that they no longer stick together and matter reduces to individual particles.


The particles I mentioned at the beginning, which make up the contents of the Universe – everything we are made of - only make up about 5% of all matter. That means, 95% of the matter in the Universe is unidentified. This is called Dark Matter. Very little is known about dark matter hence it is an active field of research.
